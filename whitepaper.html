What it is

A 3D browser visualization that runs a small three-step solver in a scene framed in orbital / celestial-mechanics language. A central object sits at the middle of the scene. Several “observer satellites” are positioned on orbital paths around it. Each observer sees a different view of the central object determined by where on its orbit it currently is. The solver aligns the observers’ partial views, then runs a series of backward refinement passes that visually converge on a stable reconstruction at the centre — rendered as a brief “supernova” flash when the convergence completes.

The orbital framing serves two purposes. First, it is aesthetic: a 3D scene with orbiting bodies is more visually legible than an abstract graph. Second, it is structural: orbits give a natural way to position multiple observers at distinct, geometrically related positions around the same object, with the geometric relationship between observer frames captured directly by the orbital parameters.

The whole demo is a single self-contained HTML file with no build step, no server, and no external dependencies beyond a modern browser. WebGL is required for the 3D rendering.

Why it matters — bio digital twins

A bio digital twin is a computational model of a specific biological system — a section of cortex, a hepatic lobule, a heart chamber — wired tightly enough that it can predict the system’s behaviour under a stimulus. Many of the most useful biological twins involve multiple sensors looking at the same underlying tissue from different geometric vantage points. A cardiac MRI is multiple sensor coils each picking up a partial projection of the same heart. A multi-electrode array on a brain slice is many electrodes each picking up a partial signal from the same volume. A panel of biomarkers in blood serum is many measurements each reflecting a partial projection of the same metabolic state.

The orbital solver makes this multi-vantage-point reconstruction problem geometrically explicit. Each observer satellite is one sensor; its orbital position determines what it sees; the snap-lock step is the geometric alignment of the observer frames into a consistent global reference; the backward refinement passes are the iterative reconstruction of the central object from the aligned partial views. The visualization is small enough to fit in a browser tab and 3D enough to make the geometry tangible in a way the cellular and grid demos do not.

The framing for the trio (generator + cell simulator + scanner) is the trio white paper. This visualization is not part of the trio — it is a standalone 3D geometric instantiation of the framework’s multi-observer reconstruction question, paired with interstellar-harmony (same encode/process/decode shape on a flat ranked grid) and decoder-of-the-encoder (the BWV 847 fugue version).

How it works — three steps in 3D

Step 1 — Propose problem

A central object is laid out in the 3D scene. Several observer satellites are placed on orbital paths around it. Each satellite has a position on its orbit (parameterised by orbital phase) and a view direction pointing toward the central object. The view each satellite has is determined by its current orbital phase: same object, different angle. The visual rendering shows all satellites simultaneously with their view cones drawn as faint cones intersecting the central region.

Step 2 — Snap lock (eigenvectors)

The geometric alignment step. The solver computes an eigenvector decomposition of the inter-observer covariance matrix and uses the leading eigenvectors to align all satellite frames into a consistent global reference. Visually, the satellites snap into a more orderly configuration — orbital orientations adjust slightly, and the view cones converge on a single coherent target volume. The snap is a discrete moment in the animation: before, the geometry is approximate; after, it is locked.

Step 3 — Back-pass refinement (×27)

Twenty-seven backward refinement passes run the reconstruction through successive corrections. Each pass takes the current estimate of the central object’s structure, projects it forward through each satellite’s view geometry, compares the projection with what the satellite actually sees, and back-propagates the residual through the inverse geometry to update the central estimate. Twenty-seven was chosen empirically to produce a visually clean convergence on the demo’s example geometry.

When the back-passes complete, the central object has reached a stable reconstruction. The “supernova” flash that appears at this moment is a visual cue marking the stability — the reconstruction is no longer changing tick by tick. The flash has no physical or astrophysical meaning; it is animation marking the structural convergence.

Reading the visualization

When you open the live demo:

  • The 3D scene appears with the central object and several orbiting satellites.
  • Press through the three steps in order (the demo auto-advances or accepts manual stepping depending on configuration).
  • Step 1: the scene is rendered with all satellites in their initial orbital positions; view cones are drawn but not yet aligned.
  • Step 2: the snap is visible as a discrete adjustment of the satellite frames; cones converge.
  • Step 3: backward passes refine the central reconstruction; the central object becomes increasingly defined as passes accumulate; the supernova flash signals stable convergence.
  • The orbit can be paused, stepped, and (in some versions) rotated by the user to inspect the geometry from different camera angles.

What to look for:

  • Geometric framing of multi-observer. Each satellite is a partial projection. The orbital geometry makes the relationships between observer frames spatially obvious.
  • Snap as eigenvector alignment. The discrete jump in step 2 is the moment a consistent global reference becomes possible from the inconsistent per-satellite frames. This is the geometric analogue of “approximate orthogonality” in the framework.
  • Iterative refinement, not a single shot. The 27 back-passes are explicit; convergence is gradual. The visualization makes the iteration visible, which most pedagogical inverse-problem demos do not.

Companion work

This visualization sits with two repositories that explore the same multi-observer reconstruction question on different substrates:

  • interstellar-harmony — same encode/process/decode shape on a flat ranked grid (rank-as-time geometry).
  • decoder-of-the-encoder — Bach BWV 847 fugue rendered as a navigable graph tree (musical-decomposition framing).

All three demonstrate the same conceptual question — how do partial views align into a coherent reconstruction? — on three different substrates: 3D orbital scene, flat ranked grid, navigable musical tree.

What is not in the paper

The honest list of what this paper deliberately does not claim:

  • A working gravitational-lensing model. The orbital framing borrows the geometric intuition (each observer sees a refraction of the central scene determined by its position) but does not implement actual lensing physics. There is no metric tensor, no general-relativistic ray tracing, no mass distribution affecting the geometry. Real gravitational-lensing inverse problems are a separate field of computational astrophysics.
  • An astrophysics tool. The “supernova” flash is animation, not a physical event. The demo’s geometry is chosen for visual legibility, not for any astrophysical relevance.
  • A norm-growth claim. Earlier prose around this family of demos asserted that the aggregate of many partial views had bigger norm than the original signal — that ∥R(f)∥ > ∥f∥. That inequality has been retracted in the v0.1 draft. The current claim is conditional and weaker: under four hypotheses and a bounded computational budget, the aggregate exposes structural features that are not accessible from the original signal alone within the same budget. The orbital solver is a 3D geometric playground for the multi-observer reconstruction shape, not a proof of the conditional claim.
  • A general inverse-problem solver. The 27 backward passes converge on the demo’s chosen example geometry. They are not a general-purpose method; real inverse problems in 3D imaging require regularisation, validated forward models, and careful numerical conditioning that the demo does not provide.
